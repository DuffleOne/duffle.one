How to work with me

A practical guide to help you collaborate with me with less guesswork.

What is a user guide? A quick definition (expand if helpful)

A user guide (sometimes called a “Manual of Me”) is a short document describing how someone prefers to work: communication style, expectations, and the conditions where they do their best work.

"They're more like guidelines."
- Geoffrey Rush, Pirates of the Caribbean

If you'd like the wider framework, see manualof.me.

About me

My name is Laura Miller, and I've been a software engineer for over fifteen years. I started programming when I was eleven, encouraged in that direction by my dad, who has a deeply technical background, and my friend Tim Rogers, who sat with me and learnt by my side while we were at school.

That early curiosity took me into the Microsoft Student Partner programme, and later into contracting directly for Microsoft. It was the beginning of my career, and it helped me see how software engineering worked in a professional setting. It also taught me that I don't love massive businesses, or the complex operational processes you have to jump through inside them.

From being a contractor to seeking full-time employment, I met James Billingham and Freddy Macnamara. Together, with a very small team, we started what would become Cuvva.com, and I took on my first true founding engineer role. That experience changed how I think about work: I realised I'm not a programmer, I'm an engineer. I like solving complex problems, and I like building entire products 🚀

I live alone in the East End, and love photography, and 3D printing.

How I work

I like a high degree of autonomy. I enjoy being part of the very high level thinking that goes into a problem before any solution is thought of. I'm very good at being creative with ideas and work very well in unstructured environments where there aren't distinct teams or separation of responsibilities.

I enjoy a role where it's quite different from week to month. I like the flexibility. A weakness I have is if given a single project for months, I will struggle and not work as well.

I really must be close to the customer, and that can mean either directly talking to the customer or being the customer. I like to dogfood the product, which means if I can't use the service that I am trying to build, I definitely don't perform as well. But that also means I seek a lot of feedback, I need feedback from you, the team around me, and the customers that we are serving, all for different purposes. I will reach out, listen and hear, and ask direct questions to get to the root of the problem.

Team dynamics

I always start as an individual contributor and try to be at the forefront of writing code and fixing problems. Due to the senior nature of the role I try to play, it always goes hand in hand with long-term strategic planning and providing a lot of information and feedback to those around me. As the company grows, I move into a more managerial role where I am doing less programming day-to-day and more management of the team, hiring, and meetings.

I find it hard to split focus between "making" and "managing", keeping the entire system in my head when programming, versus working on wider-term strategic planning. If you find me getting too specific on the detail, please prompt me to "go to manager mode", and if you find me thinking very high level without specifics, prompt me the other way into "maker mode".

Team dynamics change a huge amount in the first few years of a business, and I will attempt to keep my finger on the pulse of this, but feedback to speed or slow that transition. One thing I'm aware of, and ask others the same, is the maker vs manager scheduling.

Working together

What I value

  • Doing it the right way over the easy way
    The "right" way is not the same as the slow way; the easy way always has an unforeseen cost.
  • Build and act in small chunks
    Every problem I come across is broken down, it should be kept that way. Don't build it up again into a big solution to deploy in one go.
  • Consistency over perfection
    I'd rather be consistently wrong than occasionally right. Consistency is easier to debug to become right.
  • Morality and kindness
    I will struggle following a business decision that I don't feel is morally right.
  • Direct communication
    If you don't like the way I say or do something, you need to tell me in no uncertain terms. If you want something done a specific way, say that.
  • Feedback is just that
    For both giving and taking, I'll share my thoughts and feelings, and I'll listen to yours. I'll never be offended if you do not take my feedback; it should just be a single variable in many.
  • Explain yourself
    One of my greatest strengths is my ability to explain a complex system and weighted decisions in a way that is easy to understand and follow. I ask the same of others around me.

More detail

Communication preferences Slack for async; messages before calling; 24h notice for meetings.

I firmly believe Slack, WhatsApp, and other mobile messaging apps are async. I'll check them when I check them, so if you are looking for a fast response, you'll need to either tag me, or drop-call me.

If you call me out of the blue, I will be expecting someone to be dead, or a worst-case scenario. So it is always wise to drop me a message first. I hugely value being given a heads up first.

Something that's also important for meetings: if you reach out and say you want to meet, it is difficult to prioritise that in my workflow. It's always wise to reach out and say what the meeting's for and what you want to obtain from it. It gives me time to prepare and manage my context switching better.

Reflective vs. prescriptive communication is a big deal to me. I will only ever use reflective communication and I ask the same of others around me; I can get a little defensive and hurt by prescriptive communication, especially when used to give me feedback.

✗ Prescriptive (less effective)

"You have not given this your full attention and it deserves it."

✔ Reflective (more effective)

"I feel as though you have not given this your full attention, and I think it deserves it."

Meetings What I need from a meeting to make it worthwhile.

I often see formal meetings as a time sink. Too many chefs in the kitchen and nothing gets done. Having said that, they can be practical and useful when kicking off a project. I always like meetings to have a specific outcome to achieve, and I do approach meetings with a degree of cutthroat efficiency.

I'm always happy to end early once we've got what we need, and I always follow the makers vs managers scheduling rule. I have a lot of time for social chatter before and after the meeting.

I like knowing what meetings I am going to have the day before, it helps me prepare and manage my context switching better for the day ahead. So if you want some time to pair program, or work on a project together, then that's great. Otherwise please give me 24 hours' notice for a meeting you're putting in my calendar.

If you want to arrange a 1:1 (recurring) or a one-off meeting with me, please always tell me the purpose ahead of time in the message and/or the calendar invite. That includes if it's bad news or difficult feedback — a heads up helps me prepare and show up well.

If you're my manager — feedback on a project or what I'm working on

✗ Bad

"Can we have a 1:1 tomorrow? Got some bad news."

✗ Bad

"I've put 30 mins in tomorrow; we need to talk about the X project."

✔ Good

"Can we do 20 mins tomorrow? I'd like to give you some positive feedback on how the X work is going and one thing I'd like us to adjust."

✔ Good

"I've put 30 mins in for Thursday. I need to share some difficult feedback regarding the pacing of your work, and was hoping to hear your views."

If you're my colleague — want to know more about my work, or need a PR reviewed

✗ Bad

"Got 15 mins for a chat this week?"

✗ Bad

"Can we jump on a call? I have a few questions."

✔ Good

"Would you have 20 mins tomorrow or Friday? I'd love to hear how you approached the Z feature and get your take on the edge cases."

✔ Good

"I've opened a PR on [repo/feature] — could we do a short screen-share so you can review the flow and I can walk you through the tricky bits? I am blocked until you can review."

Feedback How I prefer to give and receive it.

Please do not leave feedback to any formalised structure. I despise it. Do not leave feedback to be given every two weeks or the first Thursday of the month. I need it the moment you notice it, the moment you have a thought, even if it's unsubstantiated. I want to know when you see something you like and I want to know when you see something you don't like. I want to hear about your thoughts sooner rather than later.

Hold me back after a meeting to tell me I lead that well, or give me some advice quickly, as we're about to head into the next project. I will always listen and take it on board. I do not like reading three-paragraph reports about things that happened weeks ago.

Examples beat generalities, and I'll assume good intent from you while asking you to assume it from me too.

Please share how you feel on a topic, even if it's not a traditional metric in a professional setting. There is truth in emotion. If I work alongside you and you dislike a library that I am suggesting we use, and you have no other reason than "I just don't like it" or "I don't like the way it's written", I want to know about that. That is still valid to me.

When I'm stressed Signals, and what helps.

I do tend to be quite anxious at times. I'm very good at reflecting on this and being a good communicator around what I need in that instance to reduce the anxiety and stress. Working in a position such as mine generally does have some stress that goes along with it, and that is okay.

I am less stressed when I'm involved in a project that I had great input on. If I'm picking up someone else's project, or held to arbitrary deadlines, or bound to do things I don't have a say in, then I get quite stressed. It's not a problem here and there, just if it happens repeatedly.

What helps? I need as much clarity on priorities as possible, and some encouragement helps. Then I'll take a walk and clear my head.

Personal life Life and other notes.

I am neurodivergent, so I can often miss some cues around what is going on. Don't be afraid to tell me if I'm not picking up on something, or if I'm not doing something you expected me to do.

I am not a fan of food, I don't drink alcohol, or take any caffeine. So I am not a great date 😆. I do enjoy an occasional hot chocolate, which is what I'll drink if we grab a drink.

I often work weird hours, so apologies for messages I send at odd times. Don't reply until you're ready and it's within your work hours.

I have a dog named Edie. She is a Maltese Shih Tzu, and is very old. I would love to bring her into the office, but she is very noisy and disruptive. I leave her with my parents when I am in the office.
